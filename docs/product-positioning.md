# Product Positioning

## The Problem with "All-in-One"

"All-in-one" is a weak differentiator. Notion, Craft, Capacities, Anytype, and dozens of others make the same claim. When you say "all-in-one," people hear "does everything, excels at nothing."

It describes a category, not a product. It's not memorable.

## What Memry Actually Is

Memry combines:

- **Inbox** - Quick capture
- **Journal** - Daily reflection
- **Notes** - Long-form thinking
- **Tasks** - Action management

The integration between these is the product's strength. But integration is a moat, not a marketing message.

## Positioning Strategy

### Lead with One Thing

Pick the sharpest edge - the thing Memry does better than anyone - and make that the headline. The rest becomes "and it also does X, Y, Z."

### Sell the Workflow, Not Features

Bad: "Notes + tasks + journal + inbox"

Better: "Capture what you read → turn it into action → never lose an idea"

The workflow tells a story. Features are a list.

### Let Integration Be the Surprise

When someone tries Memry for capture and discovers the journal connects to their tasks which connects to their notes... that's an "oh wow" moment. You can't manufacture that feeling if you lead with "we do everything."

## Potential Differentiators

### 1. Local-First + E2EE

- Data lives on your machine
- End-to-end encrypted sync between devices
- No server has your unencrypted data
- Notion and Obsidian Sync don't offer this

**Positioning angle:** "Your second brain, on your machine"

### 2. The Daily Workflow

- Journal in the morning (intention)
- Capture during the day (input)
- Tasks to close the loop (action)
- Notes for long-term thinking (synthesis)

**Positioning angle:** "From reading to doing, in one place"

### 3. Capture-First PKM

- Target user: "I spend time reading things on my computer and want to store them"
- Obsidian has no built-in web capture
- Notion's clipper is mediocre
- Readwise is read-only, requires export

**Positioning angle:** "Capture → Process → Act"

## Target User

Not "people who want to organize their life" - that's everyone and no one.

More specific: **People who consume a lot of content online and want to turn reading into action without switching between five apps.**

They're frustrated by:

- Tool fatigue (Notion + Obsidian + Todoist + Readwise + ...)
- Data scattered across services
- Privacy concerns with cloud-first tools
- Complexity of setting up Obsidian plugins

## What to Build Next

Before adding features, answer:

1. What's the minimum version that lets someone capture → process → act?
2. Can 10 people use it and give feedback?
3. What's stopping a v0.1 release?

The infrastructure is built (vault system, sync, editor). The question is whether to stop building and start shipping.

## The Real Decision

> "What would you regret more - shipping something that fails, or never finishing it?"

If the answer is "never finishing" - then finish. But finishing means shipping something imperfect, not building every feature imagined.
